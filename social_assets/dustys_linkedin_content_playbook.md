### Post 2 — What to text a mechanic

**Hook options:**

1. “My bike is making a weird noise” is not enough information.
2. If you text a mechanic, send these four things.
3. The best repair conversations start before the bike gets to the shop.

**Draft:**

```text
If you text a mechanic, send these four things:

1. Year / make / model
2. What changed
3. When it happens
4. One photo or short video

That’s usually enough to tell whether it sounds like:

- quick service
- diagnosis
- a safety issue
- something that needs a shop visit

It also saves everybody time.

You don’t need perfect mechanic language.
You just need useful context.

Example:

“2018 CBR500R. Chain noise started after the last ride. It gets louder when rolling. I’ll send a short video. Safe to ride or bring it in?”

That is 10x better than:

“Bike sounds weird. How much?”

Save this before you need it.
```

### Post 3 — Used-bike cold start

**Hook options:**

1. Never buy a used motorcycle that was warmed up before you arrived.
2. The cold start tells you things the seller may not.
3. Used-bike rule: show up before the engine is warm.

**Draft:**

```text
Never buy a used motorcycle that was warmed up before you arrived.

A cold start can reveal:

- hard starting
- smoke
- weak battery
- idle problems
- engine noise
- carb or fuel issues
- charging concerns

A warm bike can hide a lot.

That doesn’t automatically mean the seller is dishonest.
But it does mean you lost one of the easiest inspection moments.

If you’re buying used, ask the seller not to start it before you get there.

Then check:

VIN and title.
Tires.
Fork seals.
Chain and sprockets.
Brakes.
Leaks.
Cold start.
Test ride behavior if possible.

A pre-purchase inspection is cheaper than inheriting somebody else’s problem.

What used-bike red flag would you add?
```

### Post 4 — Apprentice / hiring mindset

**Hook options:**

1. I can teach wrenching. I can’t teach giving a damn.
2. The best motorcycle techs notice small things before they become big ones.
3. A good apprentice asks better questions every week.

**Draft:**

```text
I can teach wrenching.

I can’t teach giving a damn.

A good motorcycle tech notices the little things:

- loose bolts
- worn pads
- dry chain
- bad tire date codes
- leaking fork seals
- clutch feel that isn’t right
- a customer who doesn’t understand the estimate yet

The technical skills matter.
Of course they do.

But care is what keeps riders safe and keeps customers coming back.

If someone wants to learn this trade, I look for three things first:

1. Curiosity
2. Patience
3. Accountability

Skill grows from there.

What’s one trait you can’t train into a new hire?
```
