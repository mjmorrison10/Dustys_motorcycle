# Dusty's Motorcycle Service & Repair — Sales Engineering Playbook

**Purpose:** Convert rider inquiries into clear next steps without sounding like a salesperson. This is a technical discovery and quote-readiness system for motorcycle repair leads.

**Core principle:** Win the technical trust before asking for the job.

---

## Sales Engineering Position

Dusty's does not sell “motorcycle repair.” Dusty's sells reduced uncertainty:

- What is wrong?
- Is it safe to ride?
- What will it take to fix?
- What can wait?
- What will it cost before work starts?

The sales process should feel like diagnosis, not sales.

---

## Lead Qualification Framework

### First-pass triage questions

Ask these before promising price, timing, or safety:

1. What year / make / model is the bike?
2. What changed or what are you noticing?
3. When does it happen: idle, acceleration, braking, turning, bumps, hot/cold?
4. Is the bike currently rideable?
5. Any recent work, crash/drop, modification, or storage period?
6. Can you send one clear photo and one short video with sound if relevant?
7. Are you looking for repair, maintenance, inspection, or advice before buying?

### Lead categories

| Category | Definition | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Quick service | Known scope: tire, oil, brake pads, chain adjustment | collect bike info + parts details, schedule/quote |
| Diagnostic | symptom without confirmed cause | diagnostic visit / initial test block |
| Safety concern | brakes, tires, steering, throttle, oil/fuel leak | recommend not riding until inspected |
| Pre-purchase | buyer evaluating used bike | send checklist + inspection option |
| Custom / vintage | old, modified, rebuild, carb, project bike | staged scope and approval process |
| Not a fit | dealer warranty, emergency tow, unsupported ask | refer or explain honestly |

## Text Intake Talk Tracks

### Initial reply

```text
Thanks. Send me the year/make/model, what changed, and one photo or short video if you can. I’ll tell you if it sounds like quick service, diagnosis, or something I need to see in person before quoting.
```

### Safety boundary

```text
If it involves brakes, tires, steering, throttle, fuel, or an oil leak, I wouldn’t ride it until it’s checked. Send a photo/video and I’ll tell you the safest next step.
```

### Quote-before-wrenching

```text
I’ll look at it first and give you the plan before any work starts. If I find something else, I’ll call/text before touching it.
```

### Unknown problem

```text
Could be simple, could need diagnosis. I don’t want to guess and give you a fake price. Let’s start by checking the actual issue, then I’ll quote the next step.
```

## Technical Discovery by Service Type

### Tires

Ask:

- Front, rear, or both?
- Do you already have tires?
- Bike year/make/model?
- On-bike or loose wheel?
- Any TPMS, tubes, rim damage, or unusual size?

Risks:

- wrong tire size,
- old customer-supplied tires,
- damaged rim,
- tube/tubeless mismatch.

---

### Brakes

Ask:

- Front, rear, or both?
- Grinding, squealing, pulsing, soft lever, fluid leak?
- When did it start?
- Any recent pad/fluid work?

Safety note:

- Do not clear safety-critical brake issues remotely.

---

### Chain / Sprockets

Ask:

- Noise, slack, skipping, rust, tight spots?
- Last chain/sprocket replacement?
- Bike type and riding style?
- Can they send photo of rear sprocket teeth and chain slack?

---

### No-start / electrical

Ask:

- Does it crank?
- Battery age/voltage if known?
- Lights come on?
- Click/no click?
- Fuel pump prime?
- Any recent battery, wiring, accessory, or storage period?

Positioning:

- diagnostic first; avoid fixed quote.

---

### Pre-purchase inspection

Ask:

- Listing link?
- Bike year/make/model/mileage?
- Seller location?
- Modified or stock?
- Title status?
- Budget / buying timeline?
- Is seller open to mechanic inspection?

## Objection Handling

| Objection | What they may mean | Response |
|---|---|---|
| “How much?” | fear of getting ripped off | “I don’t want to guess and mislead you. Send the bike info and photo/video and I’ll tell you if it’s quick service or needs diagnosis.” |
| “Another shop said it needs everything.” | wants second opinion | “Ask them what is safety-critical now vs. what can wait. If you want, send me what they quoted and photos of the issue.” |
| “Can you just tell me if it’s safe?” | wants reassurance | “I can’t safely clear it from text alone, especially brakes/tires/steering/leaks. Send a video/photo and I’ll tell you the safest next step.” |
| “Can I bring my own parts?” | wants cost control | “Maybe, depending on the part. I need to know brand/source/fitment. Some customer-supplied parts create warranty or fitment issues.” |
| “Can you do it today?” | urgency | “Depends on the job and parts. Send year/make/model and what’s wrong. I’ll tell you if it’s realistic.” |
| “Why diagnosis fee?” | thinks looking should be free | “Diagnosis is the work of finding the real problem. It protects you from paying for guessed parts.” |

## Competitive Positioning

### Dusty vs. dealership

**Fact:** Dealerships may have OEM access and warranty systems.  
**Impact:** They can be expensive, slower, and more process-heavy for non-warranty jobs.  
**Act:** Position Dusty as the straight-answer independent for riders who want personal communication and quote-before-wrenching.

### Dusty vs. niche specialty shop

**Fact:** Some shops specialize narrowly in one brand/type.  
**Impact:** Great if the bike matches; less useful for all-makes households or varied rider needs.  
**Act:** Emphasize all-makes/all-eras capability.

### Dusty vs. mobile mechanic

**Fact:** Mobile mechanics can be convenient.  
**Impact:** Complex diagnosis, lifts, tools, tire work, and deeper repair may need a real shop.  
**Act:** Emphasize shop capability and proper inspection.

### Dusty vs. DIY

**Fact:** Riders can do simple work themselves.  
**Impact:** DIY mistakes on brakes, tires, chain, electrical, or fuel can get expensive or unsafe.  
**Act:** Encourage DIY for basic checks, but route safety-critical issues to inspection.

## Quote Process

1. Capture bike and issue details.
2. Decide quick-service vs diagnostic vs inspection vs custom.
3. Communicate whether the bike should be ridden or transported.
4. Give starting expectation or diagnostic next step.
5. Inspect/diagnose.
6. Quote before wrenching.
7. Get approval.
8. Notify if scope changes.
9. After completion: review request + referral ask.
